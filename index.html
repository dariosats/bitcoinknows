The Basics

What is Bitcoin?

The simplest explanation you'll find anywhere.

₿
Digital Money

Money for the internet age

Bitcoin is digital money that works without banks. You can send it to anyone, anywhere in the world, at any time — just like sending an email, but with value attached.

It was created in 2009 by someone using the name Satoshi Nakamoto. Unlike dollars or euros, no government or company controls Bitcoin. Instead, it runs on a network of computers around the world that anyone can participate in.

  • It's digital — it exists only on the internet, like your bank balance
  • It's limited — only 21 million will ever exist (like digital gold)
  • It's open — anyone can use it, no permission needed
  • It's global — works the same in every country
Why It Matters

Why do people use Bitcoin?

There are many reasons people choose Bitcoin. Here are the most common ones.

🔒

You own it — truly

When you hold Bitcoin, you control it directly. No bank can freeze your account or tell you what you can do with your own money.

🌎

Send money anywhere

Send value to anyone in the world in minutes, not days. No forms, no intermediaries, no business hours. It works 24/7, 365 days a year.

📈

Limited supply

Unlike dollars that governments can print more of, only 21 million Bitcoin will ever exist. Many see this scarcity as protection against inflation.

👀

Transparent system

Every Bitcoin transaction is recorded on a public ledger that anyone can inspect. The rules are clear and can't be changed by any single entity.

⚡

Low-cost transfers

Sending Bitcoin can be much cheaper than traditional wire transfers, especially for international payments. Layer 2 solutions make small payments nearly free.

👥

Financial access

Billions of people worldwide lack access to banking. Bitcoin only requires an internet connection, giving everyone access to a global financial system.

Simplified

How does Bitcoin work?

The big picture, without the technical jargon.

1

Get a wallet

A Bitcoin wallet is an app on your phone or computer. It's like a digital bank account that only you control.

2

Receive or buy

Get Bitcoin from an exchange, an ATM, or directly from someone else. Your wallet gives you an address to receive it.

3

Send it

Enter the recipient's address and amount. Hit send. The network verifies and processes your transaction.

4

It's confirmed

Miners (special computers) verify your transaction and add it to the blockchain — a permanent, public record.

Key Concepts

Terms you'll hear

Bitcoin has its own vocabulary. Here are the essentials.

🔗

Blockchain

A shared digital ledger that records every Bitcoin transaction ever made. Think of it as a giant spreadsheet that's copied across thousands of computers worldwide.

⛏

Mining

The process of using computers to verify transactions and add them to the blockchain. Miners are rewarded with new Bitcoin for this work.

🔑

Private key

A secret code (like a password) that proves you own your Bitcoin and lets you send it. Never share this with anyone — whoever has it controls the Bitcoin.

📦

Wallet

Software that stores your private keys and lets you send and receive Bitcoin. It doesn't actually hold coins — it holds the keys to access them on the blockchain.

⛺

Halving

Every four years, the reward miners receive is cut in half. This slows down how fast new Bitcoin is created, making it increasingly scarce over time.

💸

Satoshi (sat)

The smallest unit of Bitcoin. One Bitcoin equals 100 million satoshis. You don't need to buy a whole Bitcoin — you can own just a tiny fraction.

Common questions

Honest answers to the things beginners ask most.

The Bitcoin network itself has never been hacked and is secured by enormous computing power. However, like cash, you need to keep it safe. Use reputable wallets, enable security features, and never share your private keys. The most common risks come from user mistakes or scams, not from the technology itself.

Bitcoin is legal in most countries, including the United States, Canada, the EU, Japan, and many others. Some countries have restrictions. El Salvador even made it legal tender. Check the regulations in your specific country, as rules vary and can change.

Bitcoin mining does use electricity, and this is a legitimate concern. However, the industry is increasingly powered by renewable energy, and some miners use energy that would otherwise be wasted. The debate is ongoing, and it's worth looking at the nuances rather than just headlines.

Bitcoin was the first cryptocurrency and remains the largest by far. It has the longest track record, the most security, and the most decentralized network. Other cryptocurrencies ("altcoins") often have different goals or trade-offs. Many Bitcoin advocates focus exclusively on Bitcoin for its proven reliability.

If you lose access to your private keys (or seed phrase) and have no backup, your Bitcoin becomes inaccessible. This is why proper backup is essential. Write down your recovery phrase, store it safely offline, and consider using a hardware wallet for larger amounts.
